Singapore's pump price surge tops major economies

Absence of fuel subsidies and synchronized pump pricing leave drivers unusually exposed to the oil bottleneck caused by the Strait of Hormuz's closure

By Wong Pei Ting
Published March 22, 2026


Singapore's pump price increase of at least 41 US cents per litre over the past month is the steepest absolute rise among 53 major economies tracked by GlobalPetrolPrices.com, ahead of Australia (39 cents), Spain (28 cents) and every European economy in the dataset. Hong Kong, where petrol costs far more per litre, saw an increase only half as large.

This comes as Singapore imports virtually all its fuel and passes world oil prices through to consumers with almost no buffering. There are no fuel subsidies or price caps of the kind Indonesia and Malaysia use to absorb price shocks, and no oil stabilisation fund like those in Thailand and Vietnam.

But that is only half the story. The speed and uniformity of the increase point to something beyond free-market pass-through: an oligopoly that moves in lockstep. Data going back to 2011 shows Singapore's five fuel brands – Shell, Caltex, Esso, SPC and Sinopec – have rarely strayed more than eight cents apart. In a truly competitive market, at least one player might absorb margin during a spike to win customers. Here, none did.

On March 13, Caltex became the first brand to cross the S$3.42-per-litre threshold for 95-octane petrol that had stood since June 2022, when Russia's invasion of Ukraine sent crude prices soaring. Shell and SPC followed within days.

By March 18, four of Singapore's five fuel operators have converged at S$3.47 – five cents above the old record, with SPC trailing at S$3.46.

The shape of the surge is striking. After Russia's invasion of Ukraine in 2022, the climb to a record pump price took four months. This time, after five months flat at S$2.88, prices jumped six to eight times in 20 days, clocking a 20.5 per cent increase that was both faster and steeper than the 2022 run-up. For starters, it now costs S$29.50 more to fill a Toyota Corolla's 50-litre tank.

Singapore's consumer watchdog, the Consumers Association of Singapore (CASE), has raised an alarm on the speed and size of the pump price hikes. Speaking at CASE's anniversary dinner on March 15, CASE president Melvin Yong said petrol companies should exercise greater transparency and restraint in adjusting prices.

"Consumers understand that global events and geopolitical tensions can affect our oil markets. But they are also asking a reasonable question," he added. "When global oil prices go up, pump prices move up quickly. But when global prices fall, consumers expect these savings to be reflected just as quickly."

There is some basis to the concern. In late 2022, when Brent crude fell from about US$120 a barrel that June to US$75 a barrel by December, 95-octane gasoline prices took months to retreat from S$3.42. They briefly touched pre-war levels in late 2022 before drifting back up and settling around S$2.88 by early 2025, roughly 10 cents above where they stood before Russia's invasion.

The government has long disputed the asymmetry claim. When Parliament discussed the oligopolistic behaviour of major oil companies in 2020, Senior Parliamentary Secretary for Trade and Industry Tan Wu Meng said a drop in oil prices may not always lead to a drop in retail petrol price as many factors affect price. Factors include operating costs, land costs, duties and taxes, and discounts and rebates. He also noted that a 2017 Competition Commission of Singapore finding that refined petrol costs accounted for less than one-third of the listed retail petrol price.

For now, no one knows how long the Iran oil shock will last. Unlike the 2022 Ukraine shock, where alternative supply routes existed, the closure of the Strait of Hormuz chokes a passage carrying 20 per cent of the world's crude with no comparable detour. After Brent crude prices briefly surged past US$119 a barrel – up more than 50 per cent from pre-conflict levels – analysts raised 2026 forecasts, with some projecting prices could rally to between US$120 and US$170 if disruptions persist.

But even if crude eventually retreats, the reality is that a market structure built around five companies moving in lockstep gives none of them reason to go first.
